Key Metrics

On our solar information site, we explain that the key financial metrics of going solar are your total installed cost, expected annual bill savings, and available incentives or rebates, which together determine your net upfront cost. From there, visitors can quickly see their estimated payback period, long‑term return on investment over the system’s 20–25 year life, and the effective cost per kilowatt‑hour of their solar energy compared with staying fully on grid power.

  • Total installed cost and net cost after incentives or rebates
  • Expected annual electricity bill savings over the system’s lifetime
  • Payback period in years until the system “pays for itself”
  • Long‑term return on investment and effective cost per kilowatt‑hour compared with grid power
Cost Structure

Installing solar panels on a home or business usually involves a one-time upfront system cost (equipment plus labor), modest ongoing maintenance, and potential financing costs, all partially offset by incentives and long-term bill savings. On this site, the cost structure is broken down into:

  • Total installed system price (equipment + labor)
  • Cost per watt to compare system sizes and quotes
  • Design, permitting, and interconnection fees
  • Optional battery storage or upgrades that increase upfront cost
  • Incentives and tax credits that reduce your net cost
  • Low ongoing maintenance and occasional inverter replacement over the system’s life
Total Savings

Expected total savings from going solar depend on how much of your electricity use the system covers, your local power rates, and how those rates change over time. Over a system’s 20–25 year life, many homes and businesses can see tens of thousands in avoided electricity costs, especially in high-rate areas, with incentives further boosting net savings.

  • Estimated annual reduction in your electricity bill compared with staying fully on grid power
  • Lifetime savings over the 20–25+ year lifespan of the solar system
  • Impact of tax credits, rebates, and export payments on your net savings
  • Effect of future electricity price increases on long-term savings
  • Differences in total savings between paying cash, using a loan, or signing a lease/PPA
  • Additional savings potential from adding batteries, time-of-use shifting, and (for businesses) lower demand charges
